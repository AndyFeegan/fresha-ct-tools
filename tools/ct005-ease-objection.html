E Empathise

Acknowledge the concern without agreeing or dismissing

  • Have you...

The "But" Trap

Saying "I understand, but..." erases everything you just empathised with. The prospect hears: "I don't really care what you think."

✗ "I understand, but let me show you the ROI..."
✓ "That makes total sense. Can I ask — when you say too expensive, is it the monthly cost or more about whether the return justifies it?"
Training Tip Match their energy. If they sound frustrated, slow down. If they sound unsure, be warm. The goal is to make them feel heard before you ask anything.

A Assess

Find the real concern underneath the surface objection

Training Tip If you're not sure you've found the real concern, ask one more question. It's better to spend 30 seconds here than to Solve the wrong problem and lose the deal.

S Solve

Address the real concern with evidence, not argument

The Loop-Back Technique

If the prospect re-objects after your Solve (e.g. "Yeah but it's still a lot of money..."), don't re-solve. Go back to Assess — you haven't found the real concern yet. Ask: "Help me understand — what would 'worth it' look like for you?"

Training Tip Solve the real concern, not the surface objection. If they said "too expensive" but the real concern is ROI, show them the maths — don't offer a discount.

E End

Check the objection is resolved, then move forward with a clear next step

Training Tip Always end with a booked next step. "I'll send you some info" is not a next step — it's a dead end. Book a time, set up a trial, schedule a follow-up call.

Spot the Mistake

Click each scenario to reveal what went wrong and how to fix it.

"That's too expensive." — "I understand, but if you look at the ROI, it actually pays for itself in the first month..."
Mistake: Argued instead of empathising
Jumped straight from Empathise to Solve. Skipped Assess entirely — never found out what "too expensive" actually means to this prospect.
Better "That's a fair concern — cost matters. Can I ask: is it the monthly fee that feels high, or is it more about whether you'd see a return on it?"
"We already use [competitor]." — "Tell me about that — what do you like about them?" — "It does most of what we need." — "Well, Fresha has online booking, payments, marketing tools..."
Mistake: Skipped Assess, jumped to feature dump
The Assess question was good, but the answer ("does most of what we need") wasn't explored. What's the "most"? What's missing? The rep moved to Solve without finding the real concern — switching risk.
Better "So it does most of what you need — what's the bit it doesn't cover? And if that gap costs you clients or time, how does that feel?"
"It's not the right time." — "I totally get it, things are busy. Let me just set up a trial and you can look when you're ready." — [prospect never looks]
Mistake: Ended without a real next step
The rep empathised and skipped straight to End — no Assess (what's taking priority?), no Solve (how Fresha fits around it). "Look when you're ready" is not a commitment.
Better "Totally understand — what's taking priority right now?" Then solve around it: "Most of our clients set it up in 20 minutes during a quiet afternoon. Could we book a slot next Thursday to do it together?"
